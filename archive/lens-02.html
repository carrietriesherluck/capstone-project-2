Biological / Neurological Lens

Before reading becomes a habit, the brain has to win a tiny battle for attention.

Research Claim

Social media is shrinking the attention conditions that reading depends on.

Reading is declining not only because books compete with screens, but because endless scrolling changes what the brain finds rewarding. When attention becomes fragmented, a slow page begins to feel boring before the story has a chance to become meaningful.

Short-form feed → Reduced sustained attention → Reading feels harder

Eye Movement Report

Scanning Pattern

Scanning creates much larger eye movements: F and Z shapes, with abrupt pauses.

The eyes search for targets and visual anchors instead of processing text sequentially for meaning.

Scanning is efficient, but it cannot support deep comprehension or memory formation.

Result Rapid information extraction detected.

Deep Reading

Sustained attention. Sequential meaning-making.

Reading processes words in localized, linear patterns.

Mira reread the letter because the first sentence did not sound like her brother. It was too careful, too polished, too unlike the person who used to leave jokes in the margins of grocery lists. On the second reading, she noticed the smudge beside the date and realized he had written it in a hurry, trying to hide fear inside neat handwriting.

Developing Brain

The prefrontal cortex remains highly plastic until the mid-twenties.

Because this region anchors sustained attention, focus, and self-control, prolonged short-form media exposure can be especially amplified in children and adolescents. If attention control weakens early, reading becomes harder to sustain.

Natural Reward Threshold

The brain adapts to overstimulation.

The algorithmic feed operates on an unpredictable reward schedule: the next swipe might reward you, so anticipation continues. Overstimulation can elevate the brain's natural dopamine threshold, making slower biological rewards like reading feel less exciting.

Part 04

Now try reading this with audio

Hybrid literacy pairs visual reading with audio support, helping the brain process the same meaning through more than one pathway.

The river moved slowly under the bridge, carrying little pieces of moonlight between the stones. Liora listened until the water sounded like a voice, then read the sentence again and finally understood why the traveler had refused to leave.

The library was quiet enough for Mina to hear the rain tapping against the windows. As the words moved across the page, the narrator's voice helped her hold each image a little longer: the locked garden, the silver gate, the friend waiting with a lantern. Reading and listening together made the story feel less like homework and more like entering a room.

Easier to read?

This is because audio and text can stimulate the auditory cortex and the prefrontal lobe at the same time. While the auditory cortex supports sound processing, the prefrontal lobe helps organize attention and understanding, so the brain can move through information more fluently.

Audiobooks, or reading while listening to audiobooks, can make reading more immersive and more fun. This is not a replacement for deep reading; it is a hybrid literacy support that helps attention reconnect to meaning.

Part 05

The Illusion of Newness

Infinite scroll is powered by FOMO: the feeling that the next post might be newer, better, or more valuable than the thing in front of you.

new post loading someone replied watch next newer content almost refreshed

Novelty is not the same as meaning.

Infinite scroll creates an illusion of novelty. Because new content is constantly arriving, the brain starts treating "new" as "valuable," even when the information is shallow.

This is why scanning feels productive: the eyes are chasing anchors, images, bullets, captions, and signals of importance. But when eye movement is driven by visual and spatial heuristics instead of linguistic meaning, reading can become mindless. The eyes move, but processing decouples from understanding.

The result is ghost knowledge: familiarity without deep comprehension.
